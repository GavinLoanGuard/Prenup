Why the Right Attorney Matters

Not all family lawyers
handle prenups equally.

A prenuptial agreement is one of the few legal documents you'll sign that has to be bulletproof years — sometimes decades — after it was drafted. The difference between an agreement that holds up in court and one that gets thrown out often comes down to one thing: how it was written and by whom.

Prenup law falls under family law, but it's a specialized corner of it. An attorney who primarily handles divorces and custody matters may not have the same depth in drafting airtight prenuptial agreements as someone who focuses on them regularly. The implications of using the wrong attorney can be severe: a court can invalidate the entire agreement if it wasn't properly disclosed, witnessed, executed, or drafted to comply with your state's specific version of the Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA) — or equivalent statute.

"The right prenup attorney is the one who understands your state's law, your specific assets, and knows when to push back — on your behalf."

What makes a prenup enforceable?

Courts look at several factors when evaluating whether a prenuptial agreement will hold up. Your attorney needs to get all of these right:

  • Full financial disclosure — both parties must fully disclose assets, debts, and income. Hiding anything is grounds for invalidation.
  • No duress — the agreement must be signed well before the wedding. Last-minute signings are frequently challenged and often overturned.
  • Independent legal counsel — both parties ideally have their own attorney. At minimum, both must have the opportunity to seek advice.
  • Fair and reasonable terms — a wildly one-sided agreement ("you get nothing") is more vulnerable to being struck down for unconscionability.
  • Proper execution — the agreement must be in writing, signed, and witnessed or notarized according to your state's specific requirements.

How prenup law varies by state

This is the reason generic online prenup templates are dangerous. Every state has its own rules governing what a prenuptial agreement can and cannot do, how it must be executed, and under what circumstances courts will set them aside. California, for example, has some of the strictest prenup laws in the country, requiring a 7-day review period. New York courts apply a reasonable disclosure standard. Texas is a community property state with distinct rules around separate property characterization.

When PrenupAttorney matches you with an attorney, we route based on your state specifically — so you get someone who knows the local statute, local case law, and local court tendencies.

Specialization in family law

Your attorney should practice primarily in family law — not general practice with family law on the side. Prenups require depth in marital property law.

Experience in your state

State law governs prenups entirely. An attorney licensed and experienced in your state knows what language courts have upheld — and what they haven't.

Clear fee structure

A good prenup attorney gives you a clear estimate upfront. Ask about flat fees vs. hourly. Most prenups can be quoted with a range before work begins.
