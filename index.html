What is a Prenup?

Most people have it backwards.

A prenuptial agreement isn't a sign of doubt — it's a sign of financial maturity. Here's what it actually does, and why more couples are choosing one.

"A prenup is the one legal document that protects both of you equally — not just one."
01

It defines what's yours, mine, and ours.

A prenup establishes which assets and debts each person owns going into the marriage, and which are considered shared. This matters enormously when one partner has savings, property, a business, or debt the other doesn't share.

02

It protects both people — not just the wealthy one.

Prenups aren't just for the rich. They can protect a partner who earns less, preserve inheritance rights, guarantee support obligations, and prevent one spouse from being burdened by the other's pre-existing debt.

03

It replaces your state's default rules with your own.

Without a prenup, state law decides who gets what in a divorce. Those rules may not reflect your relationship. A prenuptial agreement lets you and your partner write the rules together, with full transparency.

04

It cannot cover everything — and that's okay.

A valid prenup cannot determine child custody or support — courts decide those. It also cannot waive spousal support in a way that leaves someone on public assistance. Your attorney will tell you what's enforceable in your state.

What is a prenuptial agreement, exactly?

A prenuptial agreement — commonly called a prenup — is a legally binding contract entered into by two people before they marry. It sets out how assets, debts, and financial matters will be handled during the marriage and in the event of divorce, separation, or death. In most states, it must be in writing, signed by both parties, witnessed, and executed without duress well before the wedding date.

Do I really need a prenup if I don't have a lot of assets?

Prenups aren't just for the wealthy. They're valuable if either partner carries significant student loans or other debt, if one partner earns considerably more, if either expects an inheritance, if one or both partners own a business, or simply if you want clear, documented expectations heading into the marriage. A prenup can also protect a lower-earning spouse by guaranteeing support obligations.

Can a prenup be challenged or thrown out?

Yes — which is why how it's drafted matters enormously. Prenups can be challenged on the grounds of duress (signed too close to the wedding), lack of full financial disclosure, lack of independent legal counsel, unconscionability (wildly one-sided terms), or improper execution. The safest prenups are signed well in advance, with both parties represented by separate attorneys, and with complete financial disclosure from both sides.

How far in advance should we do a prenup?

Most attorneys recommend starting the process at least 3–6 months before your wedding date. Signing a prenup within days or weeks of the ceremony dramatically increases the risk it will be challenged as signed under duress. Give yourselves adequate time for negotiation, independent review, and revision.

What can a prenup NOT cover?

A prenup cannot predetermine child custody or child support — courts will always act in the best interest of the child at the time of divorce, regardless of what any agreement says. It also cannot waive spousal support in a manner that would leave one partner dependent on public assistance, include illegal provisions, or be used to encourage divorce. Anything related to personal lifestyle choices is generally also unenforceable.

What's the difference between a prenup and a postnup?

A prenup is signed before marriage; a postnuptial agreement is signed after. Both serve similar purposes and both are legally enforceable in most states — though postnups face slightly higher scrutiny since the parties are already legally committed to each other. If you missed the prenup window, a postnup is a legitimate and increasingly common alternative.

Do both of us need separate attorneys?

Not legally required in most states, but strongly recommended. If only one party had an attorney and the agreement later gets contested, the fact that the other party had no independent legal advice is a significant strike against its enforceability. Many attorneys now require that both parties at least be given the opportunity to retain separate counsel before signing.
