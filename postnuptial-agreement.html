What is a postnuptial agreement?

A postnuptial agreement — often called a "postnup" or "post-marital agreement" — is a legally binding contract entered into by two people who are already married. It functions almost identically to a prenuptial agreement: it defines how assets, debts, property, and financial matters will be handled if the marriage ends in divorce, separation, or death.

The key distinction is timing. A prenup is signed before the wedding; a postnup is signed after. While this sounds like a minor detail, it has real legal implications — courts apply somewhat higher scrutiny to postnuptial agreements because the parties are already legally bound to each other, which changes the negotiating dynamic.

"A postnup isn't about the marriage failing. It's about making a financial agreement that reflects who you both actually are — now, not who you were when you got engaged."

Why couples get postnuptial agreements

The reasons couples seek postnuptial agreements are varied — and often rooted in life changes that happen during a marriage, not before it. Some of the most common triggers include:

A significant inheritance or financial windfall. If one spouse receives a large inheritance, a business payout, or a stock windfall during the marriage, a postnup can clarify whether those funds remain separate property or become marital assets.

Starting or acquiring a business. Business valuations are notoriously complex in divorce. If one spouse launches or acquires a business after the wedding, a postnup can set out how that business will be valued and divided — or protected — if the marriage ends.

A significant career change. One spouse stepping back from their career to raise children, pursue education, or support the other's career creates an economic imbalance that a postnup can address directly — guaranteeing support or sharing arrangements that reflect the sacrifice made.

Relationship repair. Some couples pursue postnups following a period of difficulty in the marriage — infidelity, financial conflict, or a significant breach of trust. Formalizing expectations can sometimes be a meaningful step in rebuilding.

Missed the prenup window. Sometimes couples simply didn't get around to it before the wedding. That's the most straightforward reason — and it's entirely valid.

Is a postnuptial agreement enforceable?

Yes — in most states. But enforceability requires careful attention to how the agreement is drafted and executed. Courts are generally willing to uphold postnuptial agreements when both parties had independent legal representation, there was full and transparent financial disclosure from both sides, the agreement was not signed under duress or coercion, the terms are not grossly one-sided or unconscionable, and the agreement was properly executed under your state's applicable law.

A handful of states have specific statutes governing postnuptial agreements; others apply general contract principles with family law overlay. Your attorney will know the specific requirements in your jurisdiction.

What the postnup process looks like

Once you're matched with an attorney through PrenupAttorney, the typical postnuptial process follows this sequence: an initial consultation to understand your goals and assets; preparation of a full financial disclosure schedule for both spouses; drafting of the agreement by your attorney; review by your spouse and their independent attorney (if they choose one); negotiation of any terms; signing, witnessing, and notarization; and filing with your state if required.

The entire process typically takes 3–8 weeks depending on complexity and how quickly both parties can agree on terms.
