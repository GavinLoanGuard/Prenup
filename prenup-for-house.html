Property & Real Estate Prenups

Your home is
your biggest asset.
Protect it clearly.

Whether you own a home before the wedding or plan to buy one together, a prenuptial agreement can define exactly what happens to that property — no ambiguity, no courthouse battles later.

Scenario 01

You own a home before marriage

Your property is currently separate. Without a prenup, marital contributions (mortgage payments, renovations) can convert it to marital property over time. A prenup locks in its separate status.

Scenario 02

Buying a home together before the wedding

Who contributed how much? Who owns what percentage? A prenup alongside the deed defines contribution clearly and avoids disputes if things change.

Scenario 03

One partner has investment properties

Rental income, appreciation, equity — all of these can become marital assets. A prenup can define how investment property is treated, valued, and divided.

Scenario 04

Family home or inherited property

Property received as a gift or inheritance is typically separate — but it can lose that status. A prenup explicitly protects it from being characterized as marital property.

Why Property Is Complex

The quiet way homes become marital property.

One of the most common and costly surprises in divorce proceedings involves real estate that was meant to be "separate property" — owned by one person before the marriage — slowly becoming a shared marital asset without anyone intending for it to happen. This is called transmutation, and it happens more often than most homeowners realize.

If your spouse makes mortgage payments using joint funds, renovates the kitchen, or is added to the deed — even partly — the separate property status of that home can be weakened or eliminated entirely. Without documentation of the original agreement and intent, courts may treat the appreciated value of the home as marital property subject to division.

"A prenup for property isn't complicated legal machinery. It's a single conversation, written down properly, that saves years of dispute later."

What a property prenup actually covers

A well-drafted prenuptial agreement addressing real estate should specify which property each party owned prior to marriage and its characterization as separate property, how mortgage contributions from marital funds will be handled (reimbursement vs. equity share), what happens to appreciation — does it remain separate or become partly marital, how the property will be handled in the event of divorce (buy-out formula, sale, right of first refusal), what happens if both parties live in a separately-owned home — rights of occupancy, mortgage sharing, maintenance, and how jointly purchased property is titled and split.

  • Current ownership documented — market value, mortgage balance, equity at time of marriage
  • Contribution rules — if your spouse pays toward the mortgage, does that create equity rights?
  • Appreciation allocation — does growth in the home's value stay separate or become shared?
  • Disposition terms — who gets the home, how is it valued, what's the process for a buyout
  • Occupancy rights — especially important if children are involved
